Evaluation and Extension of the
Action Chunking Transformer

Jonathan Mendelson  ·  January 2026

Paper GitHub Hugging Face

Systematic ablations of ACT on a real robot arm — dataset size, model depth, action chunking — plus a YOLO state-augmentation extension that exposes the limits of small-data IL.

scroll

Abstract

We evaluate the Action Chunking Transformer (ACT) — an imitation learning policy that predicts short action sequences (chunks) from robot demonstrations using a CVAE-Transformer architecture — on two real pick-and-place tasks with a SO-101 arm, ablating dataset size, model depth, chunk size, and temporal ensembling.

Data quantity dominates: doubling demos (48→96) lifts car success from 58% to 85%. Larger models hurt without more data. ACT fails sharply on out-of-distribution positions — and training longer doesn't fix it.

We extend ACT with YOLO state augmentation — injecting detected object pose (u, v, θ) into the state vector — but the augmented policy underperforms baselines on both tasks. Failure modes are concrete and addressable.

ACT in Context

Where the Action Chunking Transformer sits — and how it works.

ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers, Zhao et al. 2023) is an imitation learning policy for robot manipulation. Trained purely on human teleoperation demonstrations — no reward function, no simulation — it predicts short sequences of joint-angle targets (action chunks) directly from camera images and proprioception, and executes them on the robot.

It is compact enough to train from tens of demonstrations on a single GPU, making it the go-to baseline for low-resource real-robot settings. In this work ACT is trained and evaluated using LeRobot on a SO-101 6-DoF arm.

How It Works

01

Architecture

ACT CVAE architecture Copy act-cvae.png → docs/assets/img/

Figure: Zhao et al. (2023)

A Conditional VAE encoder (active only during training) takes the full demonstration trajectory and compresses it into a latent style variable z that captures movement intent. A Transformer decoder then attends to the current observations — 6 joint angles plus RGB frames from two cameras processed by a ResNet-18 backbone — and, conditioned on z, autoregressively decodes a sequence of K target joint angles in one forward pass. At inference, z is sampled from the learned prior N(0, I).

02

Action Chunking

Instead of predicting one action at a time, ACT predicts a chunk of K consecutive actions per forward pass. Step-by-step behavioral cloning accumulates errors — a small mistake in one action corrupts all that follow. Chunking breaks this dependency: the policy "commits" to a short plan, dramatically reducing compounding error. The chunk size K is a key hyperparameter; in this work we ablate K ∈ {25, 50, 100, 150}.

03

Temporal Ensembling

Temporal ensembling diagram Copy chunking+te.png → docs/assets/img/

Figure: Zhao et al. (2023)

At every timestep, overlapping chunks are queried and their predictions are blended with exponentially decaying weights — older chunks contribute less. The final action is aₜ = Σ λⁱ ât−i with λ = 0.01, yielding smoother execution.

Parameter Ablations

We vary one factor at a time across four experiments, evaluating directly on the physical robot.

01

Effect of Dataset Size

How much does the number of demonstrations matter? ACT is an offline IL method — it memorizes the training distribution. We train on 25%, 50%, and 100% of available episodes per task.

TaskFractionSuccess

Key finding: Doubling the car dataset from 48 to 96 episodes raises success from 58% → 85%. Data quantity is the dominant performance driver — the gains are superlinear, not incremental.
02

Effect of Model Size

We vary Transformer depth across Small (4,1), Medium (6,2), and Large (8,4) encoder/decoder layers while holding the dataset fixed. Larger models need more VRAM and reduce batch size.

SizeParamsVRAM
Small (4,1)43M0.9 GB
Medium (6,2)68.8M1.4 GB
Large (8,4)103M2.1 GB
Key finding: Medium (6,2) improves over Small on both tasks, but Large (8,4) collapses sharply (Car: 0.625→0.354; Pen: 0.600→0.100). The additional model capacity is not supported by the small dataset — the same pattern as any ML scaling law.
03

Action Chunk Size & Temporal Ensembling

We sweep chunk size K ∈ {25, 50, 100, 150} with no TE, and compare the best against a temporal-ensembling (TE) policy with λ = 0.01. All runs use the full dataset per task.

K=25 — Oscillation failure

K=25: insufficient look-ahead → robot oscillates without progress.

K=100 — Successful rollout

K=100: optimal chunk size for both tasks, consistent with ACT paper recommendation.

Key finding: K=100 is optimal for car; temporal ensembling performs best on pen but introduces a grasp-failure mode by averaging "open/close" commands. Both parameters require per-task tuning.
04

Distribution Shift: In-Sample vs. Out-of-Sample

We re-evaluate the 96-episode car model on poses never seen during training (objects placed between discrete training positions, or at diagonal orientations). We also compare 50k and 100k checkpoints to test whether overfitting causes the OOD failure.

In-sample vs. out-of-sample positions Copy in-out-sample-eval.png → docs/assets/img/

Blue: training poses. Red: OOD evaluation poses (between positions or diagonal orientations).

OOD failure — car task

Out-of-sample pose: policy fails to adapt despite converging on in-sample configurations.

Key finding: In-sample success improves from 60% → 85% with more training, but out-of-sample performance stays at 19% for both checkpoints. This is not classic overfitting — the policy simply has no coverage of unseen states.
05

Failure Modes

Five distinct failure modes were observed across all evaluation rollouts. Most share a common root: the policy encounters states absent from the training distribution.

Mode Collapse

Robot reaches an intermediate pose and freezes — stuck between two motion modes from the training data.

Video

Oscillation Loop

Robot shakes/vibrates repeatedly around a fixed position without making progress toward the goal.

Video

Action Drift

Robot slowly wanders off-course over the episode — cumulative error in long action horizons.

Video

Grasp Failure

Robot misses the object, applies wrong force, or drops it after grasping. Common with temporal ensembling.

Video

Recovery Failure

After making an error, the robot cannot self-correct — no recovery demonstrations were in the training set.

YOLO State Augmentation

Can we improve out-of-distribution generalization without collecting more data?

85.4% In-sample (car)
→
19.4% Out-of-sample (car)

The best ACT model drops from 85% to 19% on unseen configurations. Collecting more demonstrations is expensive. We ask: can explicit object-location signals close this gap automatically?

Approach

YOLO-augmented ACT: OBB centroid coordinates (u,v,θ) from the top camera are appended to the joint state, forming an extended observation fed to ACT. Dashed line = standard RGB path unchanged.

Inspired by visual prompting work that overlaid bounding boxes on ACT's image input, we extend this idea in two ways: (1) inject object locations into the state vector rather than the image, bypassing ResNet for this signal; (2) replace human annotation with an automated YOLOv8-OBB detector.

A dedicated YOLOv8s-OBB model is fine-tuned on 200 annotated frames per task. At inference, it runs on the top camera at each timestep and appends detected source/target centroids to the joint-state vector before ACT.

Failure Analysis & Proposed Fixes

Failure ModeExplanationProposed Fix
Detection noise & jitter OBB centroids jitter between frames; occasional missed detections create sparse, noisy signal. Feature dropout during ACT training; reject low-confidence detections; replace YOLO with a more stable model.
Redundancy with visual stream ResNet already encodes the camera image. YOLO provides the same information in a different format — potentially conflicting signals. Ablate by removing ResNet and relying on OBB state only, to test the contribution of explicit coordinates.
Sparse joint-state coverage With 50 episodes, the joint distribution of (joints, YOLO coordinates) is thinly populated. Collect more diverse demonstrations.
Higher-dimensional state, same data Adding OBB coordinates expands the observation space without adding data — mirrors the Large model collapse in Experiment 2. Collect a higher volume of demonstrations proportional to the expanded state dimension.
